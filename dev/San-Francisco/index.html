Rally for Privacy this Sunday in San Francisco

Join thousands across the country in rallying for privacy and against NSA surveillance.

This Sunday is 8/4. We're declaring it "1984 Day."

Location: 12pm at Embarcadero Plaza, San Francisco

Speakers: Daniel Ellsberg, Mark Klein, Jennifer Granick, Norman Solomon, and Cullen Hoback

Share this page

Speakers

Daniel Ellsberg

Danniel Ellsberg is a former US military analyst, author, and activist. Mr. Ellsberg famously leaked the Pentagon Papers to The New York Times in 1971, revealing the systematic deception of four presidencies.

Jennifer Granick

Jennifer Granick is Director of Civil Liberties for the Center for Internet and Society at Stanford Law School. Jennifer Granick has written extensively about NSA surveillance, including a recent piece in the New York Times.

Mark Klein

Mark Klein is the AT&T/NSA whistleblower who revealed in 2006 that AT&T was secretly cooperating with the NSA to divert communications to the intelligence agency, leading to the Hepting v. AT&T lawsuit.

Norman Solomon

Norman Soloman is an author journalist, politician, media critic and founder of the Institute for Public Accuracy.

Cullen Hoback

Cullen Hoback is Director of "Terms and Conditions May Apply," a documentary exploring the loss of online privacy

Why we're rallying

NSA surveillance is unconstitutional

Recent revelations about the NSA's surveillance infrastructure have revealed that phone and email records of US and foreign nationals are being collected and stored by the government.

The level of surveillance occurring presently is an abuse of the rights guaranteed by the Fourth Amendment and the United Nation's Universal Declaration of Human Rights.

Privacy matters and should not be compromised

Privacy is not simply a privilege, but a necessary precondition for a free and democratic society. In the words of Benjamin Franklin, “Those who would give up essential Liberty, to purchase a little temporary Safety, deserve neither Liberty nor Safety.”

We expect our government to protect our right to privacy with respect to digital communications data.

Restoring the Fourth Amendment

We ask that our representatives in Congress to restore the privacy guaranteed to US citizens by restoring the Fourth Amendment and passing legislation to revoke the NSA's ability to conduct mass surveillance of US and foreign citizens.
